Size

Hognose snakes don’t get very big: around three feet long for females and one and a half to two feet long for males. Hognoses don’t take up that much space: you can house females in a twenty gallon tank and males in a ten gallon tank comfortably.

Temperament

Hognose snakes are great to own because they’re very calm and don’t move very fast. However, they can be a little picky with their food and difficult to get on a rodent diet.

Head

Hognoses are named for their upturned noses, which they use for digging. They’ll dig around in the dirt and leaves to find their favorite food: frogs and toads.

Many people believe that snakes are deaf, but they do actually have ears! They’re just underneath a layer of skin so it’s muffled, like if you held your hands over your ears all the time.

Tongue

Hognoses flick their tongues to scent the air and ground to detect prey and predator. Smell is their best sense.

Hognoses are technically venomous, but as they are not harmful to humans (and rarely bite anyways), they are considered nonvenomous.

Eyes

Hognoses have fat little bodies and round little eyes. Like a lot of snakes, they have poor eyesight and identify shapes but not details. They mostly rely on smell.

Hognoses like to burrow through the substrate––not much need for good eyesight when you’re living under the surface.

Tail

Hognose snakes are most well known for their defense mechanism: when they’re intimidated, they’ll ‘hood up’ and puff out their cheeks. If that doesn’t work, they’ll roll over on their backs, open their mouths, and pretend to be dead!

If you try to flip them back over, they’ll go immediately back on their backs to assure you that they really are dead.
